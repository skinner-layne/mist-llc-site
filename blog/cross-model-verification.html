The Hallucination Nobody Else Catches

April 7, 2026 8 min read Mist Dynamics

A lawyer filed a brief that cited cases that did not exist. The model produced clean Bluebook-style citations, plausible quotations, and confident legal reasoning. It looked right until someone checked. In a demo, that kind of failure is embarrassing. In production, it is liability. The same pattern shows up outside law: a financial summary that moves a decimal point and turns millions into tens of millions, a clinical workflow that suggests the wrong dosage, a research paper citing a DOI that does not resolve. The problem is not that language models sometimes say strange things. The problem is that they say wrong things fluently enough to pass review.

Most vendors in the AI infrastructure space respond to this problem with some version of "we use multiple models" or "we have a verification layer." When we started building Mist Dynamics, we thought the same. We thought that sending the same prompt to three architecturally different models and looking for disagreement would catch most hallucinations. It turns out that is wrong. This post is about why the naive cross-model story does not hold up, and what we actually built instead.

What we found when we tested cross-model verification

We ran the obvious experiment. We took a set of factual questions — both easy ones and hand-curated tail facts that we expected to trip up individual models — and we sent each question to three different architectures: OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. We measured two things. First, how often the three models disagreed on the answer. Second, whether the disagreement cases correlated with wrong answers.

The result was humbling. Across every question we tested, the three models produced the same answer. Agreement rate: effectively one hundred percent. Disagreement rate: zero. The frontier models are saturated on factual recall — if a fact is in the training data of a major model, it is in the training data of all of them, and they retrieve it reliably. Our cross-model verifier had nothing to verify.

That is the honest result. Cross-model diversity, by itself, does not catch hallucinations on the class of questions most enterprise buyers will first try. If you only need a system that returns correct answers to factual questions, any single frontier model is already good enough, and paying three times as much to ask three of them is waste.

But that is not the problem we are solving

The questions that actually fail in production are not the questions where the model knows the answer. They are the questions where the model does not know, but produces a fluent confident answer anyway. The lawyer's hallucinated citation was not a retrieval failure — it was a fabrication. The model did not look up an existing case and get the citation wrong. It synthesized a case that never existed, with a plausible name, a plausible court, and a plausible year, because the prompt asked for one and the model was trained to produce plausible text. Cross-model diversity does not help here. If the prompt pushes all three models into the same fabrication mode, they will fabricate the same plausible lie, and they will all agree.

We call this class of failure shared hallucination. It is the single most dangerous thing a production AI system can do, because every traditional safety mechanism fails against it:

  • Single-model confidence scores do not catch it, because the model is confident.
  • Self-consistency (sampling the same model multiple times) does not catch it, because all samples share the same prior.
  • Cross-model voting does not catch it, because the other models share overlapping training data and produce the same fabrication.
  • RAG does not catch it if the retrieval layer returns nothing and the model falls back on its priors.
  • "Are you sure?" prompting does not catch it, because the literature shows self-reflection is an unreliable signal at best.

Shared hallucinations are not edge cases. They are the exact failure mode behind most of the AI incidents that have made the news — the Avianca case, the fake academic citations, the invented company policies, the fabricated historical events. Each of these incidents happened because a confident fluent wrong answer passed every automatic check and landed in front of a human who trusted it.

The two layers we actually built

Catching a shared hallucination requires two signals, not one. You need to detect the fabrication structurally — looking at the output itself for statistical signatures of confabulation — and you need to detect it across architectures, so you can distinguish "this single model is wrong" from "all models are wrong in the same direction." Mist Dynamics runs both layers on every verified query.

Layer one is Prism. Every verified prompt is dispatched in parallel to three models from three distinct providers: OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. Not three samples from one model — three architectures with different training lineage, different post-training methods, and different failure modes. We get back three responses, along with a full audit trail of which model said what, when, with what latency and cost. This layer, on its own, gives you the evidence production you need for compliance, the architectural failover you need for availability, and the consensus signal you need for confidence labeling. It does not, by itself, catch shared hallucinations. That is not its job.

Layer two is QGE. QGE is our in-house hallucination detector. It does not require external documents. It does not require RAG. It does not ask the model whether it is sure. It analyzes the structure of the output itself — grammatical coherence, internal consistency, statistical signatures that distinguish retrieved content from fabricated content. On HalluBench, the standard hallucination detection benchmark, our most recent evaluation showed a 26.1 percentage point improvement in mean detection over the published state of the art. We are tracking to a 95 percent precision at 95 percent recall target on the next generation of the detector, tuned specifically for the high-stakes regime where false positives cost almost as much as false negatives.

On its own, QGE can flag suspicious output from a single model. But flagging a problem is only half the job. When QGE says "this looks like hallucination," you still need an answer to give the user. That is where the two layers combine.

The decision matrix that catches what nothing else catches

Every Mist verified query ends up in one of four buckets. The cell in the middle is the one that matters:

Prism agreement QGE score Interpretation What Mist does
All three agree Low Legitimate consensus. Models know the answer and retrieved it reliably. Return the answer with a high-confidence label.
All three agree High Shared hallucination. The dangerous case. Flag, return "I do not know" honestly, log the case for review.
Models disagree One low, others high Single model got it right, others hallucinated individually. Return the low-score answer, log the disagreement.
Models disagree All high Question is outside training coverage. Return "I do not know," recommend human review.

The second row is the thing nothing else catches. Every single-model hallucination detector fails it because it only sees one output. Every voting-based ensemble fails it because voting rewards agreement. Every RAG system fails it if retrieval returns nothing. Mist catches it because QGE sees the structural signature of fabrication, and Prism confirms that all three independent architectures produced that fabricated content — which is a very strong signal that the models share a bad prior and cannot be trusted on this particular query.

When we detect a shared hallucination, we do not try to fix it. We return "I do not know." That is the defensible answer. A system that knows when it does not know is worth more than a system that is usually right.

Who needs this and who does not

You need Prism plus QGE if your product is in a regulated industry, if your customers include lawyers or doctors or auditors or researchers, if the cost of one confident wrong answer exceeds the cost of three model calls plus a hallucination detector, or if your procurement team has "multi-model verification" and "hallucination detection" as named controls in your vendor contract.

You do not need this if your use case is ideation, brainstorming, creative writing, internal search, or anywhere "close enough and fluent" is the target. Those workloads should run on single-model routing, which Mist also provides, at a much lower cost.

We do not recommend verification everywhere. We recommend it where error has real cost. Customers configure per endpoint which prompts get verified, what the cost ceiling is, and what happens when a hallucination is flagged.

What we are not claiming

We are not claiming zero hallucinations. No system can honestly claim that, and anyone who does is selling you something.

We are not claiming that Prism improves accuracy on simple factual questions. We tested this and the benchmarks showed zero delta against single-model output. Frontier models are already good at factual recall. Our value is not in the easy case.

We are not claiming that QGE catches every hallucination. It catches most, at a defensible precision-recall point, and we publish our benchmark numbers. Hallucinations that pass QGE and are structurally indistinguishable from retrieved content still happen, and a serious buyer should assume they exist.

We are claiming that the combination of Prism and QGE catches a class of failure — shared hallucinations — that no single-layer system can catch, and that this failure mode is behind most of the production AI incidents that actually hurt people and businesses. If that is the problem you have, Mist is the only infrastructure stack that addresses it directly. If it is not, you should use something cheaper.

Verification is not magic. It is an engineering discipline applied to a substrate optimized for plausible language. The difference between a single model speaking confidently and an architecture that knows when to say "I do not know" is the difference between a liability you hope nobody notices and a system you can actually deploy.
